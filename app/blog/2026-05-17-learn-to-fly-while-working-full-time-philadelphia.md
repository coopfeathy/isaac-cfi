# Learning to Fly While Working Full-Time in Philadelphia: A Realistic Timeline

*by Isaac Prestwich, CFII — owner of Merlin Flight Training at KPNE*

The single most common question I get from prospective students at Merlin is: *"Can I actually do this with a full-time job?"*

The answer is yes. About 80% of my students in the Philadelphia area work full-time — engineers, attorneys, healthcare workers, sales reps, teachers, business owners. Here's what the realistic timeline actually looks like, what falls apart for people who try to rush it, and how to plan around your day job.

## The short answer

A working adult flying **twice a week** at a Philly-area flight school can earn a Private Pilot Certificate in **6–9 months**.

A working adult flying **once a week** typically takes **10–14 months**.

If you can only fly once every 2–3 weeks, expect **18–24 months** — and budget for extra hours because of memory loss between lessons.

Below is the breakdown.

## The math: 60 hours of flight time

FAA Part 61 requires **40 flight hours minimum** to qualify for the Private Pilot checkride. The reality is most students take **55–70 hours** before they're ready. I plan all my students for 60 hours.

Flying twice a week, at 1.5 hours per flight on average, gives you **3 flight hours per week**. Divide 60 by 3 and you get **20 weeks** — roughly 5 months of pure flight time.

Add to that:

- Ground school hours (running parallel; about 30 hours over 5 months)
- Two stage checks before the checkride
- A few weeks of weather cancellations (this is the Philly area, not Arizona — plan for 25% weather loss)
- A 2-month checkride prep phase
- A few weeks waiting for the FAA Knowledge Test result and scheduling the practical exam

End-to-end: **6 to 9 months** for the twice-a-week student.

## Where students who work full-time get stuck

I've watched dozens of students try this. The ones who finish on time have all done the same six things. The ones who quit or stretch to 18 months have all made the same mistakes. Here are the patterns:

### 1. Pick a flight school close to home

If your school is more than 30 minutes from your house, you will start canceling lessons. The 2-hour drive + 2-hour lesson + 30-minute debrief eats your entire evening or half your Saturday. Add bad weather and a long drive becomes "why am I doing this."

Most of my students at KPNE drive 15–35 minutes. The closer-the-better rule applies more than any other factor in finishing your training on time.

### 2. Lock in a recurring weekly slot

Don't email week-to-week to schedule. Pick the same two slots every week (e.g., Tuesday 6 PM and Saturday 9 AM) and put them on your calendar as recurring events. Treat them like a doctor appointment.

This sounds obvious. It's the #1 thing that separates 6-month students from 18-month students.

### 3. Use weather cancellations for ground school

Bad weather means you can't fly — but it's a great time to knock out 2 hours of FAA Knowledge Test prep with your instructor. If your school cancels the flight portion and doesn't offer ground time, you've lost the day for free. If they swap it for ground school, you've gained progress.

We always offer to swap weather cancels into ground time at Merlin. It's how my students get through the written exam faster.

### 4. Take the FAA Knowledge Test early — not late

The Knowledge Test (written) is required before the checkride. Most students wait until the end of training to take it. **Take it at hour 20 instead.** The 70-question multiple-choice exam covers a lot of theoretical material that's actually easier to study before you've absorbed too much in-the-airplane experience. Knock it out early, never think about it again.

Test cost: $175. Locations in the Philadelphia area include PSI testing centers in King of Prussia and Cherry Hill.

### 5. Schedule your medical exam *before* you start flying

The FAA Medical Certificate has a 4–6 week lead time end-to-end (MedXPress application → AME exam appointment → physical exam → certificate issued). If you start training before getting your medical and it turns out you have a condition that requires special issuance, you've wasted 3 months of training time.

Get the medical first, then start flying.

### 6. Don't take vacation breaks in the middle

A two-week vacation in the middle of primary training is fine. A two-week vacation when you've only flown 12 hours total is brutal — you'll lose half of what you've learned and have to retrain it. If you can, front-load the vacation before training starts or back-load it after the checkride.

## A typical week at Merlin for a working student

Here's what a realistic week looks like for one of my Philly-area students:

- **Monday evening:** Read 1 chapter of the Jeppesen Private Pilot Manual at home (40 min)
- **Tuesday 6:00 PM:** Drive to KPNE (15 min for most), pre-flight brief (15 min), 1.0–1.5 hr flight, debrief (15 min). Home by 9:30 PM.
- **Wed–Fri:** Live your normal work week. 30 min total reviewing the post-flight notes from Tuesday.
- **Saturday 9:00 AM:** Drive to KPNE, brief, 1.5–2.0 hr flight, debrief. Home for lunch.
- **Sunday:** Off, or 1 hour of self-study on whatever the next lesson covers.

That's about 5–6 hours per week of total time investment, including drive time. Across 6 months that's ~140 hours of work. Realistic and doable next to a 40-hour week, if you protect those time blocks.

## The schools that work for working adults

Three operational features matter when picking a school as a working adult:

1. **Evening + weekend availability.** If they only schedule weekdays 9-to-5, you're done before you start.
2. **Same instructor every flight.** Continuity makes a huge difference. If you bounce between 4 CFIs, expect to add 10+ hours to your training.
3. **Realistic pacing.** Some schools cram-bill working adults into "accelerated" programs that promise PPL in 30 days. Don't do this with a full-time job. You'll burn out, fail, or pass and never feel like a real pilot.

At Merlin Flight Training, we run a 1-on-1 model — you have one instructor (me, Isaac) for the duration of your training, and we schedule around your work calendar. We're based at Northeast Philadelphia Airport (KPNE) with full evening and weekend availability.

## Cost over the timeline

If you finish in 6 months, the total spend is the same as if you finish in 18 months — about **$13,000–$14,000** for the full Private Pilot Certificate. Faster doesn't cost more; slower doesn't save money. The aircraft and instruction hours are the same.

For a detailed line-item breakdown, see [How Much Does It Cost to Get a Private Pilot License in Philadelphia?](/blog/2026-05-17-cost-of-private-pilot-license-philadelphia).

The only thing that costs more is taking *gaps* in your training. Lose 6 weeks of consistency and you'll re-train 10 hours of material. That's $2,000 you could've kept.

The honest first step isn't to commit $14,000 — it's to spend 90 minutes at the airport and decide if this is something you actually want to do for the next 6–12 months. A discovery flight at Merlin is $150, includes a pre-flight brief, your hands on the controls in the air over Center City Philadelphia, and a debrief at the end.
